On July 21, Belgium celebrates its National Day — a country of just 11.8 million people that has quietly become one of the world's most influential nations, according to eturbonews.com.

Belgium is often described as small, but that misses the point. It is a country where global diplomacy meets village life, where medieval towns stand minutes away from European institutions, and where diversity is not a slogan but a daily reality.

Belgium may cover only 30,689 square kilometers, yet it punches far above its weight in politics, trade, culture, gastronomy, and tourism.

## A Young Nation with Ancient Roots

Belgium became an independent constitutional monarchy in 1830, but its history stretches back to Roman times and the medieval counties of Flanders, Brabant, Liège, and Hainaut.

Located between Germany, France, the Netherlands, Luxembourg and the North Sea, Belgium has always been a crossroads of Europe. Armies crossed it, merchants enriched it, artists inspired it, and today diplomats govern from it.

Its modern identity has evolved not despite its diversity — but because of it.

## Three Languages, One Country

Belgium officially recognizes three national languages: Dutch in Flanders, French in Wallonia, and German in the eastern cantons.

Brussels, officially bilingual in French and Dutch, has also become one of Europe's most international cities, where English is widely spoken alongside dozens of other languages.

Belgium demonstrates that national identity is not built on one language alone but on mutual respect, compromise, and shared institutions.

## One Country, Many Identities

Belgium is a federal state composed of regions and communities with significant autonomy.

Flanders has developed one of Europe's strongest regional brands and actively promotes itself internationally through tourism, trade, and culture. Visitors sometimes encounter Flanders almost as a destination in its own right, particularly at international tourism fairs and within global organizations such as the UN Tourism network.

Yet this diversity is not a weakness. Rather, it is part of Belgium's unique model — a country where regional identities flourish within a stable national framework.

Belgium proves that unity does not require uniformity.

## The Capital of Europe

Few countries have a greater international footprint.

Brussels hosts the European Commission, the Council of the European Union, NATO Headquarters, hundreds of international organizations, thousands of multinational companies, and one of the world's largest diplomatic communities.

On any weekday, policymakers from every continent walk the streets around the European Quarter while tourists explore nearby medieval squares only minutes away.

It is perhaps the only capital where local cafés serve both neighborhood regulars and ministers from across Europe.

## A Tourism Giant in a Small Package

Belgium offers remarkable diversity within short travel distances.

Visitors can enjoy medieval Bruges and Ghent, cosmopolitan Brussels, fashion and diamonds in Antwerp, art nouveau architecture, the rolling Ardennes forests, North Sea beaches, World War heritage sites, cycling routes through Flanders, castle-filled Wallonia, Michelin-starred dining, and world-famous breweries.

In little more than two hours by car, travelers can experience entirely different landscapes, cultures, and culinary traditions.

## Connected to the World

Belgium's infrastructure ranks among Europe's finest.

International access is exceptionally convenient: Brussels Airport connects Belgium with destinations across Europe, North America, Africa, Asia, and the Middle East. Brussels South Charleroi Airport serves numerous low-cost European routes. High-speed rail links Belgium with London via the Channel Tunnel, allowing travelers to board the Eurostar in central London and arrive in Brussels in about two hours. Paris, Amsterdam, Cologne, and Luxembourg are all easily reached by high-speed train. One of Europe's densest motorway and rail networks makes exploring the country effortless.

Belgium also offers accommodation for every traveler — from historic boutique hotels and luxury city properties to countryside castles, family-run guesthouses, and internationally recognized hotel brands.

## A Culinary Capital

Belgian cuisine enjoys a reputation far beyond its borders.

Belgium gave the world Belgian chocolate, Belgian waffles, more than 1,500 varieties of beer, world-renowned fries served with dozens of sauces, moules-frites, fine pastries, and artisanal cheeses.

Its restaurants consistently earn Michelin stars while local neighborhood cafés remain central to Belgian social life. Food here is not simply nourishment — it is culture.

## Open to the World

Perhaps Belgium's greatest strength is openness. Its ports connect global trade. Its universities attract international students. Its companies export worldwide. Its institutions shape European policy. Its citizens are accustomed to hearing multiple languages every day. Belgium has learned over generations that cooperation often achieves more than confrontation.

## Belgium's Quiet Success

Belgium rarely seeks the global spotlight. Instead, it builds consensus. It values quality over size. Innovation over noise. Practical solutions over grandstanding.

That quiet confidence has helped transform one of Europe's smallest countries into one of its most influential.

On this National Day, Belgium celebrates more than its independence. It celebrates a model of coexistence where different cultures, languages, and traditions have found a way to thrive together.

In an increasingly interconnected world, Belgium reminds us that a nation does not need to be large to have a global voice. Sometimes, the smallest countries have the biggest ideas.

Happy National Day, Belgium.
